Many teams think they have observability because they log prompts and responses.

That works until agents become multi-step, tool-using, and stateful.
Real agent observability means understanding:
- why a tool was called
- what intermediate decisions were made
- where latency, cost, or failures were introduced

Without step-level traces, debugging agents becomes guesswork.

This is the gap I kept running into while building agents, and it’s the reason I started working on AgentBasis.

The goal is to make agent behavior inspectable by default.
Step-level traces, tool visibility, cost signals, and failure paths, not just inputs and outputs.

Observability for AI agents is no longer optional, it is table stakes in real-world systems.

According to the State of Agent Engineering report by LangChain, 89% of teams have implemented some form of observability for their agents, and 62% have detailed tracing that shows every step and tool call. Teams with production agents push this even further, 94% have observability and 71.5% have full tracing.

The takeaway is clear: without visibility into how an agent reasons, how it calls tools, and how it flows through multi-step workflows, you can’t reliably debug issues, optimize performance, or build trust internally and externally.

If you’re shipping agents now or planning to, make sure you’re capturing and inspecting:
- step-by-step traces of agent actions
- tool calls and decision logic
- performance metrics that expose failures, latency, or unexpected behavior

Simple “prompt in, response out” logging will not cut it at scale. Real observability means traceability across the entire reasoning and execution pipeline.

A common question when building AI agents is whether to use raw code or agent frameworks.

Frameworks are useful for fast iteration. They handle orchestration, memory, and tool calling out of the box, which makes them great for prototyping and early experiments.

Raw code becomes important once you move toward production. It gives you control over prompt structure, retries, tool execution, latency, token usage, and failure handling. 

These details start to matter quickly at scale.

The mistake I see is keeping framework defaults in production without fully understanding the abstractions underneath.

A simple way to decide:
 Use frameworks to explore workflows.
 Use raw code or very thin abstractions when you need reliability, observability, and predictable behavior.

If you cannot trace why an agent made a decision, called a tool, or consumed a certain number of tokens, you do not really control the system.

Why 95% of AI Agents die in the Pilot?

A recent MIT report dropped a bombshell: 95% of enterprise AI pilots never make it to production. After $30B+ in investment, most companies are hitting a wall. But here’s the interesting part, the report notes that it’s NOT because the models aren’t smart enough.

The failures happen because agents:
Are "Brittle": They work in a demo but break the moment they hit real-world data noise.
Don't Learn: They don't retain feedback or adapt to context over time.
Fail Silently: They hallucinate or loop without the developers knowing why.

The "Observability Gap" is the real killer. Most teams are building agents like they build static software. But agents are probabilistic, not deterministic. If you can't trace the "thought process" of an agent, you can't debug it. If you can't see the feedback loop, you can't improve it.

The 5% who succeed aren't just using better prompts, they are doing AgentOps. They have the observability to see exactly where the "reasoning" broke down and fix it in real-time.

Stop building black boxes. Start building systems you can actually see.
